The Monopolist problem

Background

Consider two finite sets $X$ and $Y$, as well as a probability measure $\mu$ over $X$. We have in mind $X$ as a set of agents, $\mu$ their distribution, and $Y$ as a set of products sold by a monopolist to the agents. There is a value $b(x,y)$ for the agent $x$ to purchase good $y$. We assume that each agent $x\in X$ maximizes its utility, $$ \sup_{y\in Y} b(x,y) - v(y), $$ where $v(y)$ denotes the price of good $y$. Then the goal of the monopolist is to set up prices so as to maximize profits, i.e. $$ \max_{v:v(y_0)\le v_0} \int_X \big(v(T_v(x)) - c(T_v(x))\big)\,\mu(dx)=:J(v), $$ where $T_v(x)=\mathrm{argmax}_y b(x,y) - v(y)$ is the optimal map associated with prices $v$ and $c(y)$ is the manufacturing cost of good $y$. Note the presence of an *outside option* $y_0\in Y$ that the monopolist doesn't control.

The Rochet–Choné problem

The $d$-dimensional Rochet--Choné problem [1] is a specific instance of the monopolist problem with $X=[1,2]^d$, $Y=[0,\infty)^d$, the value function $$ b(x,y)=x\cdot y $$ and the manufacturing cost $$ c(y)=\frac 1 2 \lvert y\rvert^2. $$ And the outside option is located at $y_0=0$.
